Positive Discipline Techniques for When Your Child Ignores You: A Guide to Getting Heard

We have all been there: you are standing in the kitchen, asking your child to put on their shoes for the third time. They are staring at a screen or playing with Legos, and they genuinely don’t seem to hear you. It is easy to assume they are being defiant or rude, but more often than not, they are simply hyper-focused.

The goal of positive discipline isn’t to force instant obedience through fear; it is to build a habit of listening. When you shout from another room, you are teaching your child that they don’t need to pay attention until your voice reaches a certain decibel level. Here is how to change that dynamic.

Get Into Their Sightline (The “Touch and Eye Contact” Rule)

If your child is deep into an activity, their brain has effectively muted the background noise—including your voice. You can call their name ten times from the hallway, and it will likely be ignored.

Instead of raising your voice, walk over to where they are. Gently place a hand on their shoulder or knee. Wait for them to physically turn their head toward you. Once you have eye contact, deliver your request in a calm, neutral tone. By breaking their physical focus, you reset their attention. If they are in the middle of a complex task, adding a transition helps: “I see you’re building a bridge. I need you to put your shoes on in two minutes so we can head out.”

Use “When-Then” Statements

We often fall into the trap of “If-Then” statements, which sound like threats: “If you don’t stop playing, we aren’t going to the park.” Threats trigger defensiveness and often lead to power struggles.

Shift to “When-Then” phrasing. This frames the task as a logical step toward something they want. For example: “When your shoes are on, then we can get in the car to go to the park.” It removes the emotion from the request. You aren’t the “bad guy” stopping the fun; you are simply the facilitator of the next step. If they ignore you, don’t repeat yourself. Simply repeat the “When-Then” statement once more, and then wait. The natural consequence is that the fun activity (the park) is delayed by the amount of time it takes them to complete the chore.

Offer Limited Choices

Sometimes, ignoring a parent is a subconscious way of regaining a sense of control. If you bark orders, they feel pushed around. If you provide a choice, they feel like a participant.

Instead of saying, “Clean up these blocks right now,” try, “Would you like to put the blue blocks in the bin first, or the red ones?” By focusing on how they perform the task rather than whether they perform it, you bypass the urge to ignore you. You are accepting that the task will get done, while giving them the agency to decide the order of operations.

Reduce the “Background Noise”

We talk a lot as parents. We narrate, we remind, we praise, and we correct. Over time, children learn to filter out the “parent channel” because it is almost always on. If you want your child to listen, your words need to have value.

Practice being concise. Instead of saying, “Hey, how many times have I told you that your backpack needs to be by the door? It’s a tripping hazard and we’re going to be late,” try just saying, “Backpack, door, please.” When you say less, you increase the likelihood that they will hear you. If you find yourself lecturing, stop. If they didn’t hear the first short instruction, they certainly aren’t going to hear the lecture.

Follow Through Without the Anger

The most common reason children stop listening is that they have learned they don’t have to. If you ask them to clear their plate, they ignore you, and then you end up clearing it yourself because you’re in a rush, you have inadvertently taught them that your requests are optional.

If you ask for something, be prepared to wait for it. If they ignore you, move to the next logical step. If you need to leave the house and they haven’t put on their shoes, you might have to say, “I see you aren’t ready. I am going to wait in the car. I will start the engine when your shoes are on.”

This isn’t a punishment; it is a boundary. You are showing them that your requests are tied to real-world outcomes. When they see that you don’t get angry, but you also don’t back down, they eventually learn that listening the first time is the path of least resistance.

When They Still Don’t Respond

If you’ve tried the eye contact, the choice, and the “When-Then” approach, and you’re still met with silence, step back. Check in with yourself—are you calm? If you are frustrated, your child will sense the tension, which often leads to them shutting down or getting defiant. Take a breath and wait for a moment where they aren’t mid-task.

Building a habit of listening takes time, but by lowering the volume and increasing the physical connection, you turn a battle of wills into a simple, predictable routine. Once you master these subtle shifts, you’ll find that you don’t have to raise your voice to be taken seriously.

To help you put these strategies into practice, consider how you might adjust your daily routine to create more “low-friction” moments for your child. Taking the time to observe where the communication breakdowns happen is the best way to determine which of these techniques will work best for your family’s specific rhythm.
