Screen Time Contracts for Kids: A Step-by-Step Guide to Drafting Age-Appropriate Agreements

When my oldest child turned ten, the “I want a phone” conversation turned into a daily battle. I realized that my verbal promises—“you can play for an hour after homework”—were being interpreted very differently by a pre-teen than by me.

We didn’t need more nagging; we needed a document. We sat down at the kitchen table and drafted a screen time contract. It wasn’t about being a drill sergeant; it was about defining the boundaries so we could stop arguing about them. Here is how we built one, and how you can do the same today.

Why a Written Contract Works Better Than Verbal Rules

A verbal rule is a moving target. If you tell a child they have “some time” on their tablet, they hear “until I’m bored.” By putting it on paper, you turn the screen into a privilege with specific requirements rather than a default state of existence.

The goal isn’t to create a legal document that feels oppressive; it is to create a “user manual” for your home’s technology. When the rules are written down, you stop being the “bad guy” and the contract becomes the authority. Instead of saying, “Get off the game,” you can simply ask, “Does your current activity follow the agreement we signed?”

Step 1: Define the Non-Negotiables

Before you invite your child to the table, list your hard lines. For our family, the non-negotiables were:

  • Charging location: All devices must be plugged into the kitchen island charger by 8:00 PM. No devices in bedrooms overnight.
  • The “First Three” rule: Before picking up a screen, the child must complete their three daily tasks: homework, a 20-minute physical activity, and clearing their dinner plate.
  • Privacy settings: Any new app download requires parental approval, and passwords must be shared with parents.

Write these down clearly. If you don’t define these before the negotiation, you’ll end up making reactive, angry rules later that feel unfair to your child.

Step 2: The Collaborative Drafting Session

Sit down with your child when everyone is calm—never right after a fight about an iPad. Ask them what they think is fair. You might be surprised. Often, kids are more reasonable than we expect when they feel like they have a seat at the table.

If they ask for two hours of gaming, and you think that’s too much, negotiate. Maybe they get one hour of “free play” and an extra 30 minutes if they choose to spend it on an educational app or a creative project like coding or digital art. When they help define the rules, they are significantly more likely to follow them.

Step 3: Define the Consequences of Breaking the Contract

This is the part most parents skip, leading to inconsistent punishments. If the contract is broken, what happens?

Make the consequences logical. If the rule is about a specific app, the consequence should be losing access to that app for 24 hours. If they break the “no devices in bedrooms” rule, they lose the right to have a device in their room for a week. Avoid “I’m taking your phone for a month,” which is usually an emotional threat you won’t actually follow through on.

Write these consequences into the bottom of the document so there are no surprises when the rules are broken.

Step 4: Making It Age-Appropriate

A contract for a seven-year-old looks very different from one for a fifteen-year-old.

For a younger child, keep it visual. Use a checklist format with images of the tasks they need to complete before screen time. Keep the time limits short and easy to track, like “two episodes of a show” or “thirty minutes of playtime.”

For teenagers, the focus shifts to responsibility and safety. Their contract should include clauses about digital citizenship—specifically, what they should do if they encounter cyberbullying or see something inappropriate online. You are moving from managing their time to mentoring their online behavior.

How to Handle Revisions

A screen time contract is a living document. We review ours every three months or whenever there is a significant change in their schedule, like the start of a new school semester or summer break.

If your child consistently follows the rules for a month, offer a “promotion.” Maybe they get an extra 15 minutes on weekends or the ability to download one new game without asking, provided they show it to you first. This teaches them that responsibility earns them more autonomy, which is the ultimate goal of parenting.

Troubleshooting Common Pushback

If your child refuses to sign, don’t force a signature. Instead, explain that the device is a tool provided by you, and if they aren’t willing to agree to the terms of use, they aren’t ready to use the tool. Usually, the desire to have the device will quickly lead them back to the table.

When you feel like the system is failing, don’t throw the paper away. Use it as a conversation starter. Ask them, “What part of this isn’t working for you?” and adjust accordingly.

Moving forward, you can treat this process as a way to build trust rather than a way to enforce control. Once you have your baseline agreement drafted, you’ll find that the daily friction over screen time decreases significantly, leaving you more energy for the parts of parenting that are actually fun.
